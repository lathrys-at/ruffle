# Ruffle: Adaptive Calibration-Free Fusion of Heterogeneous Retrieval Channels

Ruffle fuses the ranked outputs of several retrieval channels (a fuzzy lexical-overlap engine, a text-embedding nearest-neighbour search, a CLIP text–image search) into one ranking, without per-channel score calibration and without ever comparing one channel's raw scores against another's. It is a standalone crate: it knows nothing about the channels that feed it beyond an opaque identifier per candidate and a scalar score per candidate.

The design target is the regime where calibration is impossible. The operator has no relevance labels and no way to author representative queries, because the corpus is supplied by their users. Yet the channels are on wildly different and individually meaningless scales: a cosine near 1, a log-odds in nats, an integer count. Reciprocal-rank fusion (RRF) [Cormack2009] is the standard answer there because it discards magnitudes and votes on ranks. Ruffle also departs from the score-combination metasearch family (CombSUM/CombMNZ and the score normalizations they require [FoxShaw1994; Lee1997; MontagueAslam2001]), because without labels there is no way to normalize one channel's scores against another's. Ruffle keeps RRF's scale-freedom but stops treating the channels as equally trustworthy and mutually independent. It estimates, per query and without labels, how discriminating each channel is on this query and how redundant the channels are with each other, and it weights the fusion accordingly.

Those two quantities are estimable without labels because they are dimensionless: they are properties of the shapes and agreements of the channels' outputs rather than of ground truth. An idealized minimum-variance optimum (§1) depends on the channels' noise structure only through its dimensionless part (§2). That is why a calibration-free layer can be expected to exist, and it sets the direction of the weighting. But Ruffle does not attain that optimum. It departs from the ideal in three ways. It applies weights to a rank fusion rather than to a calibrated score combination (§6). It reads each channel's discrimination relative to that channel's own history rather than on an absolute cross-channel scale (§4, §9). And it estimates only the dimensionless part of a noise structure it never directly observes (§5). What it delivers is therefore per-query adaptive, redundancy-aware weighting on top of RRF rather than optimal cross-channel-precision fusion. A first end-to-end simulation (§10) bears this out unevenly: the weighting helps materially when channels vary in informativeness per query or are redundant, roughly ties plain RRF when they are near-equal, and can cost in the adversarial purely-static corner the simulation probes (§10), when channels differ only in a fixed, persistent way the per-query reading cannot see (§9). Two of the estimators are validated by simulation below. The third, the coupling, is harder to estimate; §5 explains why and how the design handles it.

## 1. Fusion as inverse-covariance weighting

The starting point is an idealized model whose optimum motivates Ruffle's weights but is not attained by them; the gap is made precise in §2 and §6. The model treats each channel, after an unknown monotone calibration of its score to a common relevance axis, as a noisy linear readout of one latent relevance signal $`r`$, with unit loading, so every channel responds to $`r`$ with the same gain:

$$\mathbf{s} = r\ \mathbf{1} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\sim(\mathbf 0,\ \Sigma),$$

with $`\mathbf s\in\mathbb R^N`$ the calibrated channel scores and $`\Sigma`$ the channel-noise covariance. The target is a linear estimate $`\hat r = \mathbf w^\top\mathbf s`$ that is unbiased ($`\mathbf w^\top\mathbf 1 = 1`$) and minimum-variance. Minimizing $`\mathrm{Var}(\hat r)=\mathbf w^\top\Sigma\mathbf w`$ subject to $`\mathbf w^\top\mathbf 1=1`$ (Lagrangian $`\mathbf w^\top\Sigma\mathbf w - 2\lambda(\mathbf 1^\top\mathbf w-1)`$, $`\partial_{\mathbf w}=0\Rightarrow \mathbf w=\lambda\Sigma^{-1}\mathbf 1`$, fix $`\lambda`$ by the constraint) gives

$$\boxed{\ \mathbf w^\star = \frac{\Sigma^{-1}\mathbf 1}{\mathbf 1^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mathbf 1},\qquad \mathrm{Var}(\hat r)=\frac{1}{\mathbf 1^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mathbf 1}.\ }$$

This is the generalized-least-squares / minimum-variance-portfolio weighting [Aitken1935; Markowitz1952; HedgesOlkin1985]. (Generalized least squares fits a linear model whose residuals are correlated, weighting observations by the inverse of their covariance.) For diagonal $`\Sigma`$ it reduces to inverse-variance weighting, $`w_c^\star\propto 1/\sigma_c^2`$ (each channel weighted by its precision), and the off-diagonal jointly down-weights correlated channels. So the ideal fusion needs two things: each channel's precision (the diagonal) and the cross-channel covariance (the off-diagonal). These are the two estimands Ruffle will approximate. Three assumptions underlie the result, and each is treated where it matters below. (i) Unit loading: unequal channel gains $`a_c`$ (with $`s_c=a_c r+\varepsilon_c`$) change the optimum to $`\mathbf w^\star=\Sigma^{-1}\mathbf a/(\mathbf a^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mathbf a)`$, and Ruffle never recovers $`\mathbf a`$, so the boxed $`\Sigma^{-1}\mathbf 1`$ is the $`\mathbf a=\mathbf 1`$ special case (§2, §4). (ii) Known $`\Sigma`$: the optimality is exact only for a known covariance, whereas Ruffle estimates only its dimensionless part from finite data (§2, §5). (iii) A linear estimator: the optimum is a property of the linear combination $`\mathbf w^\top\mathbf s`$, but Ruffle never forms $`\mathbf w^\top\mathbf s`$; it feeds the weights to a rank fusion (§6). None of the three holds in the shipped system, so §1 motivates Ruffle's weights without specifying them.

The same target arises from the probabilistic side. If, conditioned on relevance, the channels are independent, then by Bayes

$$\mathrm{logit}P(R\mid \mathbf s) = \mathrm{logit}P(R) + \sum_{c}\log\frac{p(s_c\mid R)}{p(s_c\mid \bar R)},$$

the posterior log-odds is the prior plus the sum of per-channel log-likelihood ratios. This additive form is exact under conditional independence [RSJ1976]: it is the log-space image of the product rule for combining classifiers [Kittler1998] rather than the further-approximated sum rule. Calibration is the map $`s_c\mapsto\text{LLR}_c`$. This additive log-odds coincides with the $`\Sigma^{-1}`$-weighted combination above only under further conditions. The relevant and irrelevant classes must be Gaussian with equal covariance (otherwise the LLR is quadratic rather than linear: QDA rather than LDA, the quadratic versus linear discriminant). The relevance mean-shift must lie along $`\mathbf 1`$ (unit loading again). And that class-conditional covariance must be identified with the $`\Sigma`$ of the noise model. (The two differ by a rank-one $`\mathrm{Var}(r\mid R)\ \mathbf{11}^\top`$ term. Because it lies along $`\mathbf 1`$ it only rescales the weight and is absorbed by the normalization, so what breaks the equivalence is not that term's presence but its class-dependence: unequal class covariances.) Granting these conditions, the two views agree, and they fail in the same place: the additive form is exact only under conditional independence, and the off-diagonal of $`\Sigma`$ is the correction for when it does not hold: redundant channels' LLRs overlap, and summing double-counts. So redundancy estimation (§5) is the term that keeps the sum from double-counting.

## 2. Identifiability without labels

The weight $`\mathbf w^\star`$ is invariant to the overall scale of $`\Sigma`$: replacing $`\Sigma`$ by $`c\Sigma`$ leaves $`\mathbf w^\star`$ unchanged. So the absolute noise levels never enter. Only the dimensionless structure of $`\Sigma`$ does: the correlation matrix (the off-diagonal shape) and the channels' precisions relative to one another. A parameter count confirms there is nothing else to it. $`\Sigma`$ has $`N(N+1)/2`$ free numbers, the overall scale removes one, and the remainder ($`N(N-1)/2`$ correlations plus $`N-1`$ relative precisions) is exactly $`N(N+1)/2-1`$.

These dimensionless quantities are, in principle, readable without labels, because both are properties of the channels' own output distributions rather than of ground truth:

- the relative discrimination of a channel is how cleanly its scores separate a candidate group from the bulk, a dimensionless signal-to-noise ratio read from the shape of its score distribution (§4);
- the correlation is how much two channels' orderings agree beyond what their shared relevance signal forces, read from agreement (§5).

A separation statistic and a rank correlation are both unitless. So Ruffle never has to answer the question "is a cosine of $`0.7`$ better evidence than a BM25-derived log-odds of $`3.1`$"; it only ever compares pure numbers. This is what makes a calibration-free layer possible: the idealized optimum depends on the noise structure only through its dimensionless part, observable from score shapes and rank agreements alone. That sets the direction of the weighting; it does not guarantee the optimum. Three qualifications limit what this guarantees:

- Dimensionless is not the same as scale-free in the per-channel sense. The invariance under $`\Sigma\to c\Sigma`$ is a single global rescaling. The moment channels have different gains $`a_c`$ (steepness of response to relevance) the optimum is $`\Sigma^{-1}\mathbf a/(\mathbf a^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mathbf a)`$ and the gains enter the weights. They are not removed by an overall-scale argument, and Ruffle never estimates them. What §4's separation statistic estimates is a gain-over-noise SNR ($`\propto a_c/\sigma_c`$), which is the right quantity for ranking but not the inverse-variance ($`\propto 1/\sigma_c^2`$) the unit-loading optimum names.
- Each channel's discrimination is read relative to that channel's own history (§4), a quantity that is mean-zero by construction. So the layer expresses which channel is discriminating unusually well on this query rather than which channel is the intrinsically sharper instrument. Self-normalization that strips a channel's distribution-shape bias unavoidably strips its absolute level too (the two are entangled in one scalar), so a cross-channel level of precision is not label-free identifiable here, and Ruffle does not claim it (§9; recovering it is open work, §10).
- A second layer of scale-freedom sits underneath, but it answers a different question. The fusion itself (§6) operates on ranks, so a mis-estimated per-channel score scale cannot corrupt the final order, only the per-query weighting. (A mis-estimated weight does reorder; the robustness is to score units only.)

So the content of "calibration-free" is cross-channel-comparison-free, with a soft per-channel anchor: Ruffle never compares one channel's raw scores against another's, and the one native-unit quantity it does use (§4's good-score reference) is a per-channel declaration rather than a cross-channel calibration.

## 3. Facet correction

The derivation of §1 assumed all channels read the same latent $`r`$. They do not. A lexical-overlap channel, a text-semantic channel, and a text–image channel measure different facets of relevance; when they disagree it is frequently signal ("relevant in wording, not in depicted content") rather than noise to be averaged away. The unconstrained optimum $`\mathbf w^\star=\Sigma^{-1}\mathbf 1`$ can also assign negative weights. For two channels with variances $`\sigma_A>\sigma_B`$ and correlation $`\rho`$, the first component of $`\Sigma^{-1}\mathbf 1`$ is proportional to $`\sigma_B(\sigma_B-\rho\sigma_A)`$, which is negative once $`\rho>\sigma_B/\sigma_A`$. The optimal combination then shorts the noisier channel against the cleaner correlated one. For estimators of a single quantity that is correct; for facet channels it is destructive: it ranks by "text relevance minus image relevance" and discards the facet signal that disagreement carries.

So Ruffle uses a regularized, non-negative approximation to $`\mathbf w^\star`$: shrink $`\Sigma`$ toward its diagonal, and clamp weights at zero. (Clamping and the sum-to-$`N`$ renormalization of §6 mean these weights are no longer the unbiased minimum-variance estimator of §1.) Every conservative choice in the sections that follow (shrink toward independence, discount redundancy softly, never let a channel cancel another, assume independence when unsure) follows from this. Here the recall-safe choice is also the statistically correct one: aggressive decorrelation destroys facet signal, so the design keeps every channel and tilts weights only mildly.

## 4. Per-channel discrimination

The diagonal Ruffle needs is each channel's discrimination on this query, a signal-to-noise ratio that (per §2) is the ranking-appropriate stand-in for the optimum's inverse-variance precision rather than the same quantity. It takes two statistics to read it, because a single one has a blind spot that matters in practice. They answer different questions (whether the channel can rank, and whether it found anything good), and a channel receives weight only when both hold.

The first statistic, separation, asks whether the channel can rank at all. Model a channel's pool scores as a two-component mixture [Manmatha2001], a small relevant component elevated over a large irrelevant bulk, and read the separation of the two in units of the bulk's own scale. (Ruffle uses a robust, nonparametric top-vs-bulk ratio rather than fitting the mixture, and a signal-to-noise reading in the spirit of [ArampatzisKamps2009] rather than its score-normalization; it recovers no $`P(\text{relevant})`$.)

$$\boxed{\ D^{\text{sep}}_c \ =\  \frac{\text{(elevation of the extreme top)}}{\text{(robust scale of the bulk)}} \ =\  \frac{\bar s_{\text{top-}\epsilon}-q_{0.5}}{q_{0.5}-q_{0.1}}.\ }$$

The statistic must measure the top's elevation rather than the distribution's variance. A flat-but-wide distribution has large variance and zero useful separation; a channel that placed a few items far above a tight bulk has the separation that matters. As a ratio of score differences it is scale-invariant, so the compression of CLIP cosines does not mute it the way a variance would. (High-dimensional contrastive spaces pack almost everything into a narrow cone, a geometry unrelated to informativeness.) It is also shift-invariant, so a uniform drift in a channel's score scale leaves it unchanged. Read as a gain-over-noise ratio, this is the dimensionless discrimination the §1 optimum wants on its diagonal. But it is an SNR ($`\propto a_c/\sigma_c`$), not the inverse-variance ($`\propto 1/\sigma_c^2`$) the unit-loading optimum names; for ranking the SNR is the better of the two: it correctly credits a high-gain channel that a unit-loading model would misweight. It is purely within-query and needs no prior. To read whether this query is unusually well-separated for this channel, it is standardized against the channel's own running typical separation (a learned mean and variance, label-free, no declaration). That standardization removes the channel's distribution-shape bias, which is what lets a separation read mean the same thing from query to query, but it removes the channel's absolute level along with it. So the standardized statistic measures a per-query departure from the channel's own norm rather than a cross-channel precision (§2, §9).

The ratio is undefined when the bulk is degenerate: $`q_{0.5}=q_{0.1}`$ for an integer-count channel whose lower half is tied, or a near-zero denominator for a saturated CLIP cone, the two channel types named at the top. So the denominator is floored (widened toward the inter-quartile gap $`q_{0.5}-q_{0.25}`$, or held at a small multiple of the channel's score unit), the statistic is computed only when the pool carries a minimum number of distinct values, and a read that still comes out extreme is winsorized before it touches the baseline. A single $`+\infty`$ would otherwise permanently corrupt the streaming mean it feeds (the §7 `NaN` hazard, applied to $`D^{\text{sep}}`$). This numerical degeneracy is a validity boundary in its own right, alongside the modelling one below.

Separation has one blind spot: it cannot distinguish nothing matches from everything matches. A query against a thematically tight collection can make the whole pool score high and similar (all relevant), and separation reads "no standout, down-weight," which is wrong: the items are relevant and should surface. A flat-low pool (nothing matches) and a flat-high pool (everything matches) both have no standout, so separation alone gives them the same value.

The second statistic, absolute goodness, asks whether the channel found anything good. It resolves the blind spot by asking what separation structurally cannot: whether this query's top is good in the channel's own units, against what the channel typically surfaces. Its reference is therefore cross-query rather than within-query (the channel's typical top over traffic), and a flat-high pool, unremarkable against today's bulk, stands clearly above that typical level:

$$\boxed{\ D^{\text{abs}}_c \ =\  \frac{s^{\text{top}}_c - \mu^{\text{ref}}_c}{\sigma^{\text{ref}}_c}, \qquad s^{\text{top}}_c = \text{mean of the channel's top-}m\text{ scores (fixed count }m\text{).}\ }$$

The statistic is a fixed-count top average rather than the raw maximum $`s_{\max}`$, for two reasons. First, the maximum is a single high-variance order statistic, whereas the average of the top-$`m`$ is a more stable estimate of how good the channel's best matches are and is robust to one spurious top score. Second, depth-comparability, though less directly than it may seem. In normal retrieval a channel's pool is its top-$`k`$ of a fixed corpus, so the pool's top is set by the corpus, not by the cutoff $`k`$: a deeper pool only appends lower-ranked items and does not raise the top, so $`D^{\text{abs}}`$ read off the top is already depth-robust there, for the average and the maximum alike. (The worry that a maximum grows with $`k`$ holds only when the pool is a fresh i.i.d. sample of size $`k`$, which is not how a retrieval pool is formed.) The residual is a pool too shallow to reach the channel's true top, handled by refining $`\mu^{\text{ref}}`$ only across pools of comparable depth: a pool shallower than the fixed count $`m`$ cannot produce the fixed-count statistic at all, so it contributes nothing to the reference, while the current query's absolute read still uses the available top, shrunk by how thin the pool is. One extreme-value caveat also remains: even a fixed-count top average is mildly Gumbel-skewed (the distribution of a maximum has a long upper tail), so the Gaussian mean/variance summary of §8 is an approximation in the tail (flagged in §10).

Here $`(\mu^{\text{ref}}_c,\sigma^{\text{ref}}_c)`$ are the location and scale of the channel's good-score reference: the part of the diagonal that needs a reference in native units, and the one quantity in Ruffle best declared and then refined (§8) rather than estimated. For a known model an operator usually knows what a good score looks like, and states it as two interpretable anchors: an arbitrary query's typical top lands around $`T\approx\mu^{\text{ref}}_c`$, and a good match around $`G\approx\mu^{\text{ref}}_c+2\sigma^{\text{ref}}_c`$. Both anchors are in the channel's native units and are oriented to canonical higher-is-better at ingest, together with the scores (§7): for a `LowerIsBetter` channel a good match is a smaller native score, so $`T,G`$ are negated alongside the scores, after which $`G>T`$ and $`\sigma^{\text{ref}}=(G-T)/2>0`$ as the formula requires. This is the one place orientation must be applied to the reference and not only to the scores. Declaring $`T,G`$ is better than estimating the reference by sampling the index (a biased documents-as-queries proxy, frozen until recompute). The main reason is lifecycle: declaring removes the index-recompute-on-model-change coupling that sampling forces on the application. Self-correction is secondary: the declared value is the base case of a running summary, and observed tops refine it at rate $`1/(n_0+n)`$, with the declared pseudo-count $`n_0`$ setting how hard the operator's number holds (high for "trust me, refine slowly," low for "this is a guess, override quickly"). Unlike separation, $`D^{\text{abs}}_c`$ is not shift-invariant (a genuine score-scale drift moves it, which is correct), and a model change invalidates the reference outright, which the required semantic tag (§8) catches.

The two statistics together form one weight. They enter as soft, continuous factors rather than a binary gate, so there is no cliff. They combine conjunctively: a channel receives diagonal weight only when it can rank and has found something good, so the map $`g(D^{\text{sep}},D^{\text{abs}})`$ must be AND-like: small whenever either input is small. A product or a soft-min works; a sum does not, since it would let a high separation cover for an absolutely poor pool and re-open the blind spot $`D^{\text{abs}}`$ exists to close. The map must also be monotone non-decreasing, bounded, and continuous (§6 pins the output scale and the bound). A single query's read of either is noisy, so the map is only mildly aggressive (zeroing a channel on one noisy read is a recall risk), and each statistic is shrunk toward its own neutral point in proportion to the evidence backing that statistic: both statistics by the pool size (a thin pool makes both noisy), the separation read additionally by its own baseline's count, and the absolute read by the reference's count, which a declared reference seeds immediately through its pseudo-count. The shrinkage is per statistic rather than global, so a declared good-score reference carries the channel from the first query even while the separation baseline is still cold, which is what makes declaration worth preferring at cold start; shrinking toward the channel's own neutral point, rather than toward a cross-channel mean, respects the §3 facet premise that the channels are not exchangeable. There is one cost: $`D^{\text{abs}}`$ fires on the flat-high "everything matches" pool, where the channel is non-discriminating for ranking (its internal order is near-arbitrary), so up-weighting it is the recall-safe choice (the items are relevant and should surface) but trades a little precision by amplifying that arbitrary within-pool order. In that case $`D^{\text{abs}}`$ is answering an activation question pressed into service as a ranking weight; the conservative map keeps the trade mild. Finally, when the good-score reference is not declared (§8), $`D^{\text{abs}}`$ is unavailable until enough tops accumulate to seed $`(\mu^{\text{ref}},\sigma^{\text{ref}})`$; in that window the channel is carried by separation alone, a rougher cold start than the declared-reference path, which is why declaration is preferred.

Each statistic has a validity boundary. Separation is informative only when the pool straddles the signal/noise boundary, with relevant documents rarer than the pool is deep, so the top-$`k`$ holds both the relevant group and the highest-scoring irrelevants; realistic retrieval is squarely there. It is also subject to the numerical degeneracy guard above (a degenerate bulk makes the ratio meaningless). Absolute goodness is informative as soon as the good-score reference is sane, which a declared prior guarantees from the first query and which the fixed-count top statistic keeps comparable across pools of differing depth (§6). Without a declared reference, it is unavailable until one accrues.

A simulation shows separation tracking informativeness and staying scale-free. On a corpus of $`2\times10^5`$ documents with a few dozen relevants and a channel of tunable signal strength $`a`$, $`D^{\text{sep}}_c`$ on the channel's own top-$`400`$ pool rises monotonically with $`a`$ ($`4.7\to5.0\to8.0\to10.5\to20.4`$) and is exactly invariant under a $`\times100`$ rescale, while the standard deviation rises with $`a`$ and scales with the units.

A second simulation reproduces the case separation cannot handle, a pool where everything matches looking the same as one where nothing does, and shows that absolute goodness tells them apart. Against a good-score reference $`(\mu^{\text{ref}},\sigma^{\text{ref}})=(0.35,0.07)`$, compare three pools: flat-low "nothing matches," flat-high "everything matches," and "a few genuine hits." Separation cannot tell the first two apart (both score $`1.63`$), while $`D^{\text{abs}}_c`$ separates them cleanly ($`-1.3`$ for nothing-matches versus $`+3.0`$ for everything-matches) and agrees with separation on the discriminating pool.

## 5. Channel coupling

### 5.1 The redundancy decomposition

Consider two channels' scores $`S_c, S_{c'}`$ for a random document of relevance $`R`$. By the law of total covariance (the covariance of two variables splits into the covariance of their conditional means plus the mean of their conditional covariance),

$$\boxed{\ \mathrm{Cov}(S_c,S_{c'}) \ =\  \underbrace{\mathrm{Cov}\big(\mathbb E[S_c\mid R],\ \mathbb E[S_{c'}\mid R]\big)}_{\text{concordance}} \ +\  \underbrace{\mathbb E\big[\mathrm{Cov}(S_c,S_{c'}\mid R)\big]}_{\text{redundancy}}.\ }$$

The first term is concordance: both channels elevate relevant documents, so their relevance-conditional means co-vary. It is present even under conditional independence, and it is the agreement to keep: two channels confirming each other on what is relevant. The second term is redundancy: the residual covariance within a relevance stratum. The redundancy term, an expectation over $`R`$, is zero iff the relevance-averaged conditional covariance $`\mathbb E_R[\mathrm{Cov}(S_c,S_{c'}\mid R)]`$ vanishes; conditional independence $`S_c\perp S_{c'}\mid R`$ is sufficient but not necessary (zero conditional covariance is weaker than conditional independence, and nonzero conditional covariances can even cancel across the relevant and irrelevant strata). It becomes positive when the channels share a nuisance factor (both embeddings swayed by document popularity or length, independent of relevance). Redundancy is what must be discounted, because it is the double-counting that breaks the additive log-odds of §1.

The quantity to estimate is therefore the within-stratum covariance. The irrelevant stratum is the bulk of any corpus; within it, redundancy is the residual correlation, and shared nuisance biases are a non-independence one would want to discount, so the confound works in the design's favour: it lands in the redundancy term rather than the concordance term.

This decomposition is exact and the estimand is well-defined. The difficulty is in observing it.

### 5.2 Selection bias in the live pool

The natural estimator restricts a rank correlation to the low-scoring (presumed-irrelevant) region of the query's candidate pool, where concordance is absent and only redundancy remains. Simulation rules it out, for a reason that determines the design.

A fusion pool is the union of each channel's top-$`k`$. Restricting attention to that pool conditions on "scored highly by at least one channel," which is a collider: a variable that two causes both point into, so conditioning on it induces a spurious association between those causes. A document in the pool with a low score in one channel must have a high score in another (that is why it was admitted), so within the pool the channels are pushed anti-correlated. This is Berkson's paradox [Berkson1946], an instance of collider / endogenous-selection bias [ElwertWinship2014]. The selection-induced negative correlation swamps the redundancy signal, and the bottom of the pool, being the most strongly union-selected region, is the worst place to look.

Simulation (two channels $`S_c = a\ R + \lambda Z + \varepsilon_c`$, with $`R`$ the rare relevance indicator, $`Z`$ a shared nuisance of loading $`\lambda`$, and $`\varepsilon_c`$ independent unit noise, so the true within-irrelevant correlation is $`\lambda^2/(\lambda^2+1)`$; the pool is the union of each channel's top-$`k`$; here $`N=2\times10^5`$ documents, relevance rate $`10^{-3}`$, $`a=3`$, $`k=1000`$). The estimate behaves as:

| $`\lambda`$ | within-irrelevant corr., random sample | theory $`\frac{\lambda^2}{\lambda^2+1}`$ | full correlation on the pool |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 0.0 | $`0.00`$ | 0.00 | $`-0.63`$ |
| 1.0 | $`0.50`$ | 0.50 | $`-0.52`$ |
| 2.0 | $`0.80`$ | 0.80 | $`-0.18`$ |
| 3.0 | $`0.90`$ | 0.90 | $`0.16`$ |

On an unselected sample the within-irrelevant correlation recovers the true redundancy to two digits: redundancy is identifiable. On the top-$`k`$ pool the same redundancy reads as a strong negative number that only weakly tracks $`\lambda`$; the selection has destroyed it. (The unselected column reproduces $`\lambda^2/(\lambda^2+1)`$ to two digits across parameter choices; the pool column's magnitudes shift with $`a`$ and $`k`$ (the values shown are at the stated parameters), so the facts that matter are the negative sign and the weak-$`\lambda`$ tracking rather than the exact numbers.)

### 5.3 Estimating coupling from an anchor sample

The simulation says where to look: an unselected set. Redundancy is a property of the channels' shared biases and is, to first order, query-independent, so it need not be read from the query's own pool. Ruffle (or its host) maintains a small, fixed anchor: a set of representative anchor queries, each scored by every channel over a common, unselected candidate set (the whole corpus, or a random sample of it, never any channel's top-$`k`$), with the pairwise correlation estimated over the documents both channels actually scored. That correlation is rank-based: Spearman's $`\rho_s`$ over the both-scored items, mapped to the Gaussian-copula linear correlation by $`2\sin(\pi\rho_s/6)`$ (the exact bivariate-normal relation between the two). Ranks are forced by the design's own premise (§2, §7) that a score is meaningful only up to an unknown monotone calibration: a linear correlation on raw scores moves under a monotone rescaling (a compressed CLIP cone against a heavy-tailed lexical score attenuates it), while the rank-based estimate is invariant to any such rescaling, and under the §5.2 simulation model it recovers the same $`\lambda^2/(\lambda^2+1)`$ a linear correlation would. Anchor queries rather than bare documents matter, because a retrieval channel scores a (query, document) pair, not a document in isolation; scoring documents-as-queries would reintroduce the biased proxy §4/§8 reject. The anchor is unselected, so §5.2's collider is absent. This is computed periodically, amortized across all queries, and persisted in the baseline state (it merges like any other summary, §8). Per-query coupling refinement, if it is achievable at all against the selection problem, is deferred as open work.

The anchor must be full-scored, and each pair's correlation computed over only the items both channels actually scored. This is what lets the off-diagonal handle facets that are absent for some items (a text-only document with no image). Because the anchor is full-scored rather than top-$`k`$'d, an absent score there is unambiguous: every anchor document was scored, so nothing was truncated below a cutoff, and absence can only mean the facet does not apply to that item. Restricting a text↔image correlation to the both-present anchor items therefore estimates redundancy over the population where both facets apply (the image-bearing documents), which is the correct population. Feeding facet-absent items into that correlation as though a channel had merely "missed" them would corrupt the estimate, so the both-scored restriction is required, and a top-$`k`$'d anchor would reintroduce the censoring ambiguity §5.2 warns against.

The anchor estimator makes two assumptions, each of which can fail. (i) Query-independence. If redundancy is query-dependent (two channels redundant on navigational queries, independent on exploratory ones), a single pooled anchor correlation is variance-weighted toward the high-coupling regime and fits neither; applied to the independent regime it over-discounts, suppressing a genuine facet, which is a recall loss. So turning coupling on is not unconditionally recall-safe the way assuming independence is: it trades a slice of recall-safety for precision. Ruffle therefore caps the discount well below the raw point estimate and applies it only when the anchor correlation is stable across query strata (low between-stratum variance), degrading to independence otherwise. Stability is a between-refresh property, so the discount also requires at least two anchor refreshes: a single refresh has zero between-refresh variance by construction and demonstrates nothing, so it must not pass the stability gate vacuously. (ii) Stratum. A random anchor is dominated by the irrelevant/bulk stratum, so it measures the within-irrelevant coupling, whereas the double-counting the additive log-odds of §1 needs to correct is the coupling among the relevant documents near the top of the pool. Using bulk coupling as that correction assumes the shared nuisance couples the two strata alike, a further modelling assumption that compounds with query-independence. Both are flagged open in §10; the recall-safe default (assume independence) and the discount cap are what make shipping without resolving them acceptable.

When no anchor estimate is available, or two channels' candidate overlap is too thin for any agreement statistic to be stable, Ruffle assumes independence: zero coupling, no discount. That is the recall-safe default: no discount means fuller weights, more surviving candidates, and a downstream reranker that still sees everything.

### 5.4 Using the coupling

A regularized dimensionless covariance is assembled by scaling the anchor redundancy correlation $`R`$ (unit diagonal, off-diagonal = each pair's redundancy) by the channels' discriminations: $`\widehat\Sigma=D\ R\ D`$ with $`D=\mathrm{diag}(1/\sqrt{g_c})`$, so each channel's diagonal variance is its inverse discrimination $`1/g_c`$ (a more discriminating channel has the smaller variance). $`R`$ is shrunk toward the identity (§3), which also keeps $`\widehat\Sigma`$ positive-definite, and the operating weights are its clamped, renormalized inverse-covariance row-sums:

$$\boxed{\ \mathbf w \ =\  N\cdot\frac{\mathrm{clamp}_+\big(\widehat\Sigma^{-1}\mathbf 1\big)}{\big\lVert\mathrm{clamp}_+(\widehat\Sigma^{-1}\mathbf 1)\big\rVert_1}, \qquad \widehat\Sigma=D\ R\ D,\ \ D=\mathrm{diag}(1/\sqrt{g_c}),\ \ R=\text{redundancy correlation}.\ }$$

Equivalently, the precision is $`\widehat\Sigma^{-1}=\mathrm{diag}(\sqrt{g})\ R^{-1}\ \mathrm{diag}(\sqrt{g})`$, whose clamped row-sums are the operating weights; its diagonal is $`g_c\,(R^{-1})_{cc}`$, which reduces to $`g_c`$ at $`R=I`$. Either way the decoupled limit is $`w_c\propto g_c`$, matching §6. (Putting $`g_c`$ on the covariance diagonal instead would invert the intent, weighting by $`1/g_c`$.) This construction defines Ruffle's weights. The per-channel product form in §6 is its weak-coupling limit, exact when the off-diagonal is zero or fully shrunk away (the common regime at $`N`$ of three or four channels under mandatory shrinkage). The interpretable content is an effective number of independent channels, read as $`\mathbf 1^\top R^{-1}\mathbf 1`$ [Kish1965] from the regularized $`R`$ (gated, capped, and shrunk, i.e. the correlation the weighting actually used), a count ($`N`$ when $`R=I`$, fewer when channels correlate). It is kept separate from the discrimination weighting so it stays a count rather than mixing in $`g`$; clamping a shorted channel lowers the number actually achieved below this pre-clamp ideal. A text-semantic channel and a CLIP channel that share semantic bias count, together, as fewer than two channels' worth, while a lexical channel (a different measurement) keeps full weight. Shrinkage is mandatory for two reasons that should not be conflated: the facet reason of §3 (avoid shorting a disagreeing channel; this is Ruffle's own decision-theoretic choice, true even with infinite data) and the finite-sample reason that anchor correlations from a finite sample are themselves noisy [LedoitWolf2004]; below a minimum reliability the discount is dropped entirely, which is the recall-safe direction. This discount is symmetric (it down-weights both members of a redundant pair equally), and that has a delimited failure mode (it can transfer weight to a worse independent channel when a redundant pair differs in informativeness statically) together with a proven do-no-harm refinement and a partial label-free symmetry-break; both are taken up in §9–§10.

### 5.5 Diagnostics from set overlap

Ruffle emits two diagnostics, but both must avoid §5.2's collider, so they are computed from set membership instead of score correlation:

- confidence: the Jaccard overlap of the top-$`m`$ sets of the discriminating channels (the size of their intersection over the size of their union); high means the channels agree on which documents are relevant, a robust signal for setting reranker depth;
- conflict: high individual discrimination together with low top-set overlap, so each channel is confident about different documents. This is the frustration diagnostic: it flags the queries where the choice of fusion matters from those where everything agrees and it does not.

Set overlap is immune to the within-pool correlation distortion because it never correlates scores; it only asks whether the same identifiers appear near the top of two lists.

## 6. Weighted reciprocal-rank fusion

The fusion stays RRF [Cormack2009], keeping its scale-freedom and its bounded, rank-shaped per-channel contribution, with per-channel weights added (an extension of the original equal-trust RRF) that combine a per-query discrimination with an amortized redundancy discount:

$$\boxed{\ \mathrm{score}(d) \ =\  \sum_{c}\ \frac{w_c}{\eta + \mathrm{rank}_c(d)}, \qquad w_c \ =\  g\left(D^{\text{sep}}_c,\  D^{\text{abs}}_c\right)\cdot \mathrm{decorrelate}_c,\ }$$

where $`g(\cdot,\cdot)`$ is the AND-like, bounded, monotone map combining the channel's separation and absolute goodness (§4) and $`\mathrm{decorrelate}_c`$ is the redundancy down-weight. As §5.4 states, this factored product is the weak-coupling display form of the governing weight $`\mathbf w=\mathrm{clamp}_+(\widehat\Sigma^{-1}\mathbf 1)`$ rather than an independent definition; when the anchor off-diagonal is non-trivial the matrix solve governs and $`\mathrm{decorrelate}_c`$ is read back from it as a per-channel summary.

Three operating choices decide whether the order moves at all. First, the weights are normalized to sum to $`N`$, so the average channel carries weight $`1`$ and $`\eta`$ keeps its usual sharpness role. Second, the weights act only when the channels' level-normalized discrimination reads disperse beyond estimation noise: the sample standard deviation of the normalized reads across the present channels is compared against a configured threshold, and below it every weight is exactly $`1`$, so the fusion is plain RRF for that query. Within-noise differences are coin-flip bets whose measured expected payoff is at best zero, and the sharper the rank discount the more a wrong bet costs at the top, so the gate is what lets a sharp $`\eta`$ and per-query weighting coexist. Third, $`g`$ is bounded above. Weighted RRF does not inherit unweighted RRF's equal-influence guarantee: a channel of weight $`w_c`$ contributes at most $`w_c/(\eta+1)`$ to any document, so the cap on a channel's influence now scales with its weight. Bounding $`g`$ and normalizing the weights is what keeps a single channel from dominating the fused order, and that bounded-but-weight-scaled contribution, rather than RRF's original equal bound, is the influence guarantee Ruffle provides. Heterogeneity then follows:

- Absence. A candidate present in some channels' lists and not others is omitted from the absent channel's contribution rather than charged a worst-rank penalty. A document must not lose because a channel did not look deep enough to surface it; that is a recall loss. Absence conflates two different facts that Ruffle's inputs cannot separate: the facet does not apply to this item (a text-only document with no image) versus the facet applies but the item ranked below the pool cutoff (an image-bearing document the image channel truncated). Whether each holds is known to the application, not to Ruffle, but omit is the correct action for both: a non-applicable channel has no evidence, so the item should be judged on the channels that do apply; a censored-but-applicable item is the recall-safe case above. The two collapse to the same action, and the action is recall-safe in both, so the conflation does not move the ordinary ranking. The one case it cannot express is a censored-and-applicable item that should rank below a non-applicable one: a query where the facet was checked and found a poor match, i.e. mild evidence against. RRF adds only positive mass and never subtracts, so a wrong-facet item can go uncredited but cannot be pushed below a no-facet item. That is recall-safe (a precision leak the reranker absorbs) but a real ceiling, RRF's own rather than Ruffle's, and closing it needs both a per-item facet mask and sign-bearing calibrated evidence (§10).
- Ties. Within a channel, tied scores share their midrank (the average of the ranks the tie spans). A tie carries no ordering information, so none is invented from input order: each tied item receives the identical contribution, and a channel's per-item contributions are invariant under any rank-equivalent reordering of its list, ties included. The fused order breaks exact fused-score ties deterministically by first appearance.
- Heterogeneous depth $`k_c`$. The RRF term uses absolute rank, which is depth-robust by construction: rank $`3`$ is a strong hit whether the pool was $`5`$ deep or $`500`$. Normalizing rank by depth would conflate "deep in a large pool" with "deep in a small one." Depth otherwise enters only through estimation reliability: a small pool shrinks that channel's weight toward its own discrimination baseline (§4). It does not enter $`D^{\text{abs}}`$ through the cutoff itself: in top-$`k`$-of-corpus retrieval the pool's top is corpus-set, not cutoff-set (§4), so deepening $`k_c`$ leaves the top unchanged, and therefore leaves $`D^{\text{abs}}`$ (the fixed-count top average that reads off it) unchanged; the only depth effect is the shallow-pool edge case the comparable-depth refinement guard handles (§4).
- Non-co-extensive pools. Discrimination is computed on each channel's own returned set (no overlap needed); coupling on the anchor sample, over the items both channels scored (§5.3), not the live pools at all; diagnostics on whatever top-set overlap exists, degrading to "assume independent / low confidence" as overlap thins.

A channel that supplies ranks only, with no scores, cannot have a discrimination statistic; it degrades to a default weight and is flagged, rather than assigned a default distribution.

## 7. The score model and channel direction

A channel's native score is whatever scalar it produces. Ruffle requires only that it be convertible to a canonical real:

```rust
trait Score { fn value(&self) -> f64; }
```

with no blanket implementations for numeric types: a bare `f32` is not a score until the caller newtypes it and thereby declares what the number means. Newtypes are cheap in Rust. Internal accumulation is `f64` throughout because the baselines accumulate via streaming variance, and `f32` running moments drift measurably over millions of updates.

Whether higher or lower is better cannot be recovered from a score distribution (both orientations produce identical histograms), so any inference reduces to "agree with the other channels," which is circular and wrong for facet channels that are supposed to disagree. A channel oriented backwards ranks anti-relevantly and corrupts both the fusion and its own persistent baseline. Direction is therefore a per-channel registration fact, declared once at channel configuration,

```rust
enum Direction { HigherIsBetter, LowerIsBetter }
```

and Ruffle orients to canonical higher-is-better at ingest by negating descending channels. Negation suffices for the rank-and-separation statistics, which depend only on relative order and gaps and are preserved under a sign flip. The one exception is absolute goodness ($`D^{\text{abs}}`$, §4), whose reference is in native units and so is not sign-flip-invariant on its own; it is made orientation-safe by negating its declared anchors $`(T,G)`$ together with the scores at ingest, after which $`G>T`$ and $`\sigma^{\text{ref}}>0`$. So orientation is applied to the good-score reference as well as to the scores, and the claim "every statistic is sign-flip-invariant" holds only with that reference co-orientation. Direction belongs to the channel's stable configuration rather than the per-query input (a per-query direction could silently corrupt an orientation-sensitive baseline), and it is folded into the state fingerprint (§8) so that a direction change is detectable as a reason two states cannot be merged. (Scores must be monotonic in relevance; a "closeness to a target value" signal is not a score in this sense and must be mapped to one first. Non-finite values are sanitized at ingest, since a single `NaN` corrupts a streaming mean permanently.)

## 8. Persistent state

Everything Ruffle persists is a confidence-weighted summary: a small bag of sufficient statistics plus an effective observation count. Given that type and a count-weighted, associative merge, three operations that would otherwise be built separately collapse into one:

$$\boxed{\ \text{streaming update} \ =\  \text{prior} \ =\  \text{cross-deployment reconciliation} \ =\  \mathrm{merge}(\cdot).\ }$$

A streaming update is `merge(state, a-count-1-summary-of-this-query)`. A prior (operator-supplied) is a hand-written summary with a pseudo-count expressing how much it should be trusted, merged in as the initial state; real data overwhelms it at the rate its pseudo-count dictates. Reconciliation across deployments is `merge` over several summaries weighted by their counts. So a prior is the base case of the merge and consolidation is the $`N`$-argument case.

This identity is exact for the mean/variance summary with decay off: Chan's parallel formulas merge associatively and commutatively [ChanGolubLeVeque1983; Welford1962], so update order never matters. It weakens to an advertised approximation in two configurations the design also offers: under the decay option (below), which ties a merge to an external clock so that decay-then-merge $`\neq`$ merge-then-decay, and under the quantile-sketch upgrade (t-digest/KLL [DunningErtl2019; Karnin2016]), whose merges are approximate or mildly order-sensitive. The boxed identity holds with that boundary attached: one operation where the summaries are exact-mergeable, a bounded approximation otherwise (§10).

The first prior is the good-score reference of §4: the channel's typical-top-score location and scale $`(\mu^{\text{ref}}_c,\sigma^{\text{ref}}_c)`$, declared in the channel's native units as two interpretable anchors (typical best hit $`T`$, good match $`G`$) with a pseudo-count $`n_0`$. It is the quantity an operator tends to know for a given model ("for this CLIP checkpoint, a good match scores about $`G`$"), what the absolute-goodness statistic needs, and the base case of a running summary, declared once and then settled by observed top scores at rate $`1/(n_0+n)`$. (It is optional in the surface: an operator who cannot state $`T,G`$ omits it, and the reference is learned from early traffic, with $`D^{\text{abs}}`$ unavailable in the cold-start window per §4. Declaring it is strongly preferred because it is the one quantity that can carry the channel before any data arrives.) This replaces estimating the reference by offline index sampling: declaring known information is cleaner than reconstructing it from a biased proxy, it self-corrects as evidence arrives where the offline number was frozen, and it removes the index-lifecycle coupling (sample stored vectors, write a meta file, recompute on model change) that the sampling approach imposes on the application; a model change is now just "declare new anchors, bump the tag." A prior over the coupling, by contrast, is $`N^2`$ and usually unknown, so it defaults to independence, which is also the recall-safe default.

Some quantities merge across summaries and some do not. The robust per-query statistics of §4–§5 (medians, quantiles, rank correlations) are not sufficient statistics and cannot be pooled across summaries. The resolution is to be exact about the level of accumulation: the rich, non-mergeable computation happens fresh, per query (or per anchor refresh), and emits a handful of scalars (this query's separation and absolute goodness per channel; the anchor's correlation per pair). What persists is a mergeable summary of those scalar streams: a mean and variance via Chan's parallel formulas, exact and order-independent, which is all a $`z`$-score standardization needs. (A $`z`$-score is a value re-expressed as how many standard deviations it sits from the mean.) If robust historical standardization is later wanted ("is today above this channel's 80th percentile"), the baseline needs mergeable quantile sketches (t-digest or KLL, both designed to merge), accepting approximate-but-principled consolidation; the $`z`$-score path ships first and the sketch is a flagged robustness upgrade that trades exact merge for tail-resistance.

Ruffle versions state in two independent ways. A format version answers "can this build parse this file" (schema migration). A separate statistic fingerprint answers "were these two states measuring the same thing the same way" (which discrimination statistic, which per-channel orientation, which sketch parameters), because two states built with different statistic definitions are numerically incompatible even at one format version, and merging them silently is a quiet correctness bug. Reconciliation checks the fingerprint and refuses on mismatch.

Joining and merging need different identifiers. A channel's key is a stable join handle so that accumulation across time and deployments lands on the right channel. But identity-for-joining and compatibility-for-merging are different facts, and overloading the key onto both creates an asymmetric hazard. A key change is the safe failure: statistics become merely mislabeled, recoverable by a key rewrite or by cold-starting from prior. The dangerous case is the reverse, when the model behind a channel is swapped but its name kept. That permanently blends two distributions into one baseline that kept no record of which observation came from which model. Since the split state is recoverable and the blended state is not, uncertainty should resolve toward splitting. So each channel's summary carries an opaque semantic tag (e.g. `"clip-vit-b32-rev1"`, which Ruffle never interprets), checked for equality on every merge, and it is a required non-`Option` field, with the same compile-time enforcement `direction` gets. Making the field required guarantees the mechanism always exists and converts a model swap into a loud refusal at the next merge, and resuming from persisted state runs the same gate, so the live load-on-restart that a real model change always crosses refuses too rather than silently accumulating new-model observations into the old model's baselines. It cannot force the operator to bump the tag, so a never-changed tag defeats it as a bad key would. Three traps would quietly turn required back into optional and are prohibited: auto-defaulting the tag from the key (re-fuses the two concepts), an "unversioned" sentinel (optional in disguise), and untagged priors (a prior is a summary and so declares which model-version it is a prior for).

Merging baselines from genuinely different deployments (a different embedding model, corpus, or query mix) produces a blend that fits neither, so reconciliation is conditional. The reconcile tool computes and reports a divergence between input states before merging (a standardized distance between their per-channel summaries, taken over both the separation baseline and the good-score reference, keeping the larger; the reference lives in native units, so it is the number a silent model swap moves, while the scale-free separation read can sit still through a pure rescaling) and makes consolidation an informed choice; high divergence is the signal not to pool. Divergence stays advisory and never overrides the tag, but the two cross-check: high divergence under matching tags is the signature of a silent model swap with a forgotten bump (the live-discipline failure the tag alone cannot see, now detectable at the reconcile boundary), and low divergence under differing tags is a benign note (possibly a reset that discarded compatible state). The required tag makes the gate total; divergence makes a silent defeat of it detectable.

Maps are stored ordered (a `BTreeMap` over the channel keys) so two states with identical contents serialize identically, giving content-addressing and clean diffs. The summary merge is mathematically order-independent (Chan's parallel formulas), but f64 rounding is not associative, so reproducibility across different merge orders holds only up to f64 rounding; content-addressed identity is therefore taken up to that rounding, or pinned exactly by merging in the canonical `BTreeMap` key order rather than an arbitrary one. The cumulative summary merges exactly but never forgets, so it does not track corpus drift; a flagged decay option scales the effective count down over time, which is still the same weighted-summary merge with a decaying count, softening "exact" to "approximate" (decayed summaries carry different effective ages), an acceptable price for a recall-safe heuristic weight.

## 9. Scope and limitations

Ruffle produces relative weights rather than calibrated probabilities. Without labels it recovers the dimensionless part of $`\Sigma`$ that §2 showed an idealized optimum depends on (which channels are discriminating on this query, how redundant they are), but it cannot produce a true $`P(\text{relevant})`$, which requires calibration labels. It makes channels commensurable in the ranking sense rather than the probability sense. RRF has the same limitation.

A sharper limitation is that Ruffle's discrimination weights are within-channel and per-query rather than cross-channel and absolute. Both halves of the diagonal are read against the channel's own history (§4), so they answer whether the channel is discriminating unusually well on this query rather than whether it is the intrinsically sharper instrument. The persistent cross-channel level of precision the §1 optimum would weight by is not recovered, because the self-normalization that lets a separation read mean the same thing across queries also removes the channel's absolute level (the two are entangled in one scalar, and separating them label-free is open work, §10). So Ruffle adapts the fusion per query toward whichever channels are presently discriminating and away from redundant ones (a real signal plain RRF ignores), but it does not, and cannot here, install a fixed "trust the lexical channel 4× more than CLIP" ordering.

The per-query diagonal can move weight against plain RRF when there is no per-query signal to exploit. If two channels differ only in a fixed way (one persistently sharper), both standardized reads sit near zero on a typical query, and noise in a single-query separation can up-weight the muddier channel (doing unremarkably well for itself) over the sharper one (also doing unremarkably well for itself). A first end-to-end simulation (§10) shows this shape: weighting helps substantially when informativeness varies per query, roughly ties plain RRF when channels are near-equal, and loses to plain RRF in a purely static-differing regime. So the value case rests on two parts, each conditional. The redundancy discount is the more principled part: it is the one quantity here that is derived (§1), level-immune, and cross-channel, and it is what plain RRF (which treats every channel as an independent, equal vote) lacks. The per-query diagonal is the less principled part: real signal when channels' informativeness varies query to query, but a possible cost when it does not. The more-principled part fires least often at $`N`$ of three or four channels (coupling is amortized, dropped below a reliability threshold, and shrunk, so it often barely fires, §5.3–§5.4), while the measured win above came from the less-principled part, in a regime where per-query informativeness genuinely varied. (The redundancy discount has its own failure mode too: down-weighting a channel purely for sharing a nuisance can penalize one that is also highly informative, which is why the discount is capped and shrunk (§5.3–§5.4).) The value is thus doubly conditional, on the deployment actually having redundancy to discount or per-query variation to track. Where it has neither, the per-query diagonal contributes only noise, and the guidance is to run coupling and weighting at their most conservative (or off), so the fusion stays close to the RRF it sits on instead of drifting below it (as the static-differing simulation, §10, shows it can).

That static-regime cost need not be treated as inevitable. A focused follow-up (an impossibility analysis, a constructive search, a literature scout, and a simulation, reconciled adversarially, §10) shows the loss is an artifact of the blind symmetric discount, in three parts. A do-no-harm allocation provably removes the below-RRF loss (in the unidentifiable corner, assuming independence is the unique never-harm floor). The keep/discount asymmetry within a redundant pair is then partially recoverable label-free, not from the redundancy (which is provably level-blind) but from the relevance-agreement the discount discards (the unsupervised-accuracy-estimation route [Parisi2014; Jaffe2016]), when the redundant members have comparable score-distribution shapes and genuinely different gains. The core that remains, a truly symmetric pair (where the rank fusion needs no decision) and the cross-channel level of incommensurable-shape channels, is provably unidentifiable. So the conservative default holds, now with a proven floor beneath it and a delimited, principled route to do better where it applies; §10 carries the detail.

The frustration framing of §5.5 is purely diagnostic. Conflicting facet channels are a frustrated system, and one temptation is to resolve them with a minimum-violations consensus (a Kemeny optimum [Kemeny1959; Dwork2001], a frustrated-antiferromagnet ground state: the ranking that disagrees least with all inputs). That is the wrong tool here. Kemeny is correct when rankers are noisy views of one quantity (its consensus is the maximum-likelihood single true ranking under a Condorcet/Mallows noise model), and it would destroy the facet signal that channel disagreement carries by majority-voting it away. The treatment for facets is weighting instead of consensus; the frustration measure is kept only to tell the operator when fusion choice matters.

The effective number of independent channels (§5.4) is a finite, well-defined quantity: a property of the regularized redundancy correlation. The spin-glass phenomenology [MezardParisiVirasoro1987] that the frustration language gestures at (exponentially many metastable states, replica symmetry breaking, a glass transition) is large-$`N`$ physics and is not invoked at $`N`$ of three or four channels; reaching for it at this scale would be unjustified decoration.

## 10. Assumptions and open questions

- Coupling estimation is the central open problem. The within-stratum decomposition (§5.1) is exact and the redundancy estimand is identifiable on unselected scores (§5.2 simulation). The live-pool estimator is falsified by selection bias; the anchor-sample estimator (§5.3) is the proposed resolution and is sound on unselected data, but its first-order assumption, that channel redundancy is query-independent enough to amortize on a corpus sample, is itself unverified and should be tested against a per-query ground truth before it is trusted. A per-query coupling estimator that defeats the pooling collider is open, and may not exist; the recall-safe default (assume independence) is what makes shipping without it acceptable. Two further cautions tighten this. Amortizing also assumes the coupling is stratum-independent: the anchor measures the irrelevant/bulk stratum, but the correction is needed among the relevant documents near the top of the pool (§5.3). And turning coupling on is not unconditionally recall-safe: if redundancy is query-dependent the pooled estimate over-discounts the independent regime and suppresses a facet, a recall loss, which is why the discount is capped and gated on cross-stratum stability (§5.3). Assuming independence remains the only unconditionally recall-safe setting.
- The discrimination statistics are sound: separation tracks informativeness and is scale-free, and absolute goodness covers separation's flat-high blind spot (§4 simulations). Separation needs the pool to straddle the signal/noise boundary; absolute goodness needs the declared good-score reference to be roughly right, and an operator who over- or under-states it only shifts that channel's baseline weight until evidence corrects it, a soft self-healing error rather than a cliff. How the two statistics combine into one weight, and the mild map into it, need a handful of real queries to set; this is where over-aggression turns into recall loss, so the default is conservative.
- The facet model is the reason for non-negativity and shrinkage (§3); it is asserted rather than measured. If in some deployment the channels are near-noisy-copies of one relevance axis, the conservative weighting gives up a little quality, which is the safe direction.
- Decay trades exact merge for drift tracking (§8); the magnitude of the effective-age approximation in a merge should be simulated against the true pooled stream before decay is enabled.
- Tag discipline is enforced for presence but not for bumping (§8); the residual silent-blend path (a model changed under an unchanged tag, within a live deployment) is closed only by a future drift monitor watching recent-vs-baseline divergence in-process.
- Facet applicability is not an input. Ruffle cannot distinguish a facet that does not apply to an item from one truncated below the pool cutoff (§6), because the per-item facet mask lives in the application. Omit is correct for both in the ordinary cases, but it imposes a ceiling: a facet checked and found a poor match cannot be expressed as evidence against an item, since RRF only adds positive mass. Lifting the ceiling needs both a per-item availability mask and sign-bearing calibrated evidence, which is the §9 boundary between ranking and probability (reranker territory). Taking a facet mask is a real interface change to scope, and it only pays off once one is also willing to spend calibration to use it.
- Cross-channel precision level is not recovered (§2, §9). The diagonal statistics are self-normalized against each channel's own history, so they express per-query within-channel adaptivity rather than a persistent cross-channel ordering of precision; the level and the channel's distribution-shape bias are entangled in one scalar, and a shape-robust but level-preserving cross-channel precision estimate, which would let the weighting also reflect that a channel is intrinsically sharper, is open. Whether such an estimate exists label-free, and whether per-query adaptivity alone is the better target anyway, is the question to settle.
- Breaking the redundancy-discount symmetry: partially possible, with a proven floor and a hard core (§9). The symmetric discount is forced. The anchor-identified redundancy is a function of the shared-nuisance structure alone and carries zero information about which redundant member is more relevance-informative, so the discount cannot be made asymmetric from the redundancy estimate itself. The static-differing harm is the blind discount transferring weight to a worse independent channel; a do-no-harm allocation (discrimination-monotone within a cluster, never pushing a redundant member below an independent channel of equal-or-lower discrimination, defaulting to independence when the within-cluster order is unreliable) removes the below-RRF loss. Its floor is firm: assuming independence ($`\alpha=0`$) is the provably unique never-harm point in the fully-unidentifiable corner, with the discrimination-monotone bounded allocation the well-motivated, numerically-supported extension to the partially-identifiable case. The keep/discount asymmetry itself is recoverable in population from the concordance (§5.1) the discount discards. This is the unsupervised-accuracy-estimation problem [Parisi2014; Jaffe2016], where a redundant member's relevance reliability is identified through its differential agreement with a conditionally-independent channel. But the signal sits at amplitude $`O(p)`$ in the relevance rate, so on the full, unselected anchor at $`p\sim10^{-3}`$ with three or four rank-voting channels it is near-unrecoverable (a $`\sim 1/p^2`$ sample wall; on a corpus-sized anchor recovery is barely above a coin flip, and the rank-only invariance makes it worse). The escape is relevance enrichment: a score-based unique-information separation (decorrelate each redundant channel against its partner using the anchor regression, then read top-vs-bulk separation of the residual on the AND-intersection of the channels' pools, which lifts the local relevance rate from pool membership alone) recovers the ordering in simulation and is do-no-harm, while correctly declining when the members are interchangeable. Its open caveat is the enrichment itself: whether the AND-selection is free of its own selection bias and statistically sufficient at realistic anchor sizes and across incommensurable channel shapes is unverified, so this is an upgrade to validate rather than a settled result. The provably unidentifiable core stands: a true symmetric common cause (equal gains), where the rank fusion needs no decision anyway, and the cross-channel level of channels with incommensurable score-distribution shapes.
- End-to-end evidence is preliminary and mixed. A first integration test has now been run: a synthetic multi-channel corpus with known, differing informativeness and known redundancy, comparing nDCG / recall@k of {unweighted RRF, Ruffle-weighted RRF, oracle-weighted RRF}. It neither confirms nor refutes the design. When per-channel informativeness varies by query, Ruffle-weighted RRF improved nDCG@10 from $`0.61`$ to $`0.90`$ (oracle $`0.91`$, most of the achievable gap) and recall@100 from $`0.63`$ to $`0.71`$, with the anchor recovering the injected redundancy almost exactly. When channels are near-equal or mixed it ≈ties plain RRF. But in a purely static-differing regime (an adversarial extreme where channels differ in informativeness in a fixed way and there is no per-query variation to exploit, a corner real channels rarely occupy fully), it underperformed plain RRF, and not trivially (nDCG $`0.52\to0.35`$), for the §9 reason: the per-query diagonal has no signal to exploit there, and its noise plus the redundancy discount move weight the wrong way. The reading is that the weighting is worth its complexity where per-query variation or redundancy is real, and should be left off (or held at its most conservative defaults) where neither is. A real-corpus evaluation with labels remains the test that would settle it; this synthetic one only maps the regimes.

## 11. Crate surface

The shipped rustdoc is the source of truth for names and signatures. This section records the commitments the derivation imposes on the surface, the parts that are design rather than code.

- Registration is declaration. A channel registers once with a stable join-handle key, a required model-version tag (§8), a direction (§7), and optionally a good-score reference (§4). All four are construction facts rather than per-query arguments, and they are validated when a fuser is built: an out-of-range knob, a duplicate key, or a reference that cannot orient is a loud construction error instead of a silent degradation.
- Resuming from persisted state runs the same compatibility gate a state merge does: format and statistic version, baseline mode, and each channel's direction and tag against what the state was accumulated under. The load-on-restart boundary a real model change always crosses therefore refuses rather than blending (§8).
- A scored input lists only the items the channel surfaced, oriented to canonical higher-is-better and sanitized at ingest (§7); an unsurfaced item is absent and omitted (§6). A ranks-only channel is a stable property of how the channel is wired rather than a per-query inference, and is carried at the default weight and flagged (§6).
- The anchor is full-scored by construction: it is built through a constructor that visits every (candidate, channel) pair, so an absent score unambiguously means the facet does not apply, with no hidden top-`k` cutoff (§5.3). That the candidate set itself is unselected remains a caller precondition the type cannot check.
- One stateful call fuses a query and folds the readings back into the baselines; a stateless variant fuses against a prior without mutating it; a coupling refresh folds a full-scored anchor's correlations into the pair baselines (§5.3). The result carries the fused ranking, the weights actually used, per-channel flags and discrimination reads, and the two set-overlap diagnostics (§5.5), so a weighting decision is explainable from the result alone.
- All persistent state is one mergeable object whose single gated merge serves as update, prior, and reconciliation, returning the advisory divergence alongside (§8); a safe key-rename exists for the mislabeled-statistics case.
- The per-stage estimators (discrimination, coupling, weighted RRF, diagnostics) are also exposed as pure, composable functions, so each stage can be exercised and audited in isolation against this document's sections.

The CLI is a thin wrapper. `ruffle reconcile a.state b.state … -o merged.state --report` runs `divergence`, then `merge`, refuses on format/fingerprint/tag mismatch (reporting per-channel which merged and which were held separate), and warns on keys present in some inputs but not others rather than silently dropping them. A `ruffle rekey` subcommand supports the safe path: rewriting a renamed channel's key before merge, with divergence still run afterward as a sanity check.

Ruffle never interprets a candidate id and never sees which channel is "lexical" or "CLIP"; it knows only that the channel under a given key has a given history, so it is independent of the channels that feed it.

## 12. Summary

Fusing heterogeneous retrieval channels without labels is possible because an idealized minimum-variance optimum depends on the channels' noise structure only through its dimensionless part: relative per-channel discrimination and pairwise correlation, both readable from score shapes and rank agreements rather than from ground truth. Ruffle takes that as motivation rather than as something it attains: it estimates a regularized, dimensionless analog of the optimum and applies it to a rank fusion, so what it delivers is per-query adaptive, redundancy-aware weighting on top of RRF rather than optimal cross-channel-precision fusion (the persistent cross-channel precision level is not label-free identifiable, §9).

It reads the diagonal from two complementary per-channel statistics, each standardized against the channel's own history: a scale-free top-vs-bulk separation (whether the channel ranks unusually well here) and an absolute goodness that scores a fixed-count top of the pool against a declared, evidence-refined good-score reference (whether it found anything good; the part that catches the homogeneous-collection case separation alone misses). It reads the off-diagonal as a pairwise redundancy measured on a shared, full-scored anchor of representative queries rather than the query's own pool, because top-$`k`$ pooling induces a Berkson collider that destroys the redundancy signal, a failure shown by simulation and handled in the design. The anchor estimator's query-independence and stratum assumptions are, by the document's own account, the central open problem (§5.3, §10), and coupling falls back to the recall-safe independence default whenever it is unreliable. These two quantities are the failure modes of additive log-odds fusion: how informative each channel is here, and how much the channels double-count.

Weights are applied to a rank-based weighted RRF, keeping scale-freedom, with a bounded-but-weight-scaled per-channel contribution (rather than RRF's original equal influence, §6), absence omitted rather than penalized, and heterogeneous depth handled by absolute rank. Because facet channels disagree, every estimate is conservative (non-negative weights, shrinkage toward independence, independence assumed when unsure), which is both the statistically correct and the recall-safe choice.

All persistent state is one confidence-weighted summary whose single merge operation serves as streaming update, operator prior (the good-score reference being the first one), and cross-deployment reconciliation (exact for the decay-off mean/variance path, an advertised approximation under decay or quantile sketches), gated by a required per-channel semantic tag and cross-checked by an advisory divergence so that consolidation is safe rather than silently corrupting.

A first end-to-end test (§10) shows Ruffle helping where per-query variation or redundancy is real, roughly tying plain RRF where channels are near-equal, and costing materially in the adversarial purely-static corner where channels differ only statically.
